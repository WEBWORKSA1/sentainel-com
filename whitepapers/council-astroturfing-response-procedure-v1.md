# The Council Astroturfing Response Procedure

## A Defensible Municipal Response Framework for AI-Driven Communication Campaigns Under the Ontario Regulatory Stack

**Sentainel Compliance Research Group**
**White Paper No. 001 · Version 1.0 · May 2026**

---

## Abstract

In 2024 and 2025, Ontario municipalities began to experience a new category of harm: coordinated, AI-generated email campaigns directed at municipal councils with the intent of influencing local policy outcomes. The most fully documented public-record example is the Town of Thorold, where a climate-policy advocacy group used customized chatbots to generate mass constituent emails opposing the Town's participation in the Partners for Climate Protection program. Council voted 7-1 to withdraw. The incident is on public record through investigative reporting by Canada's National Observer (April 30, 2026) and earlier coverage by CBC and The Pointer.

The incident is not unique to Thorold. The same pattern has affected councils in Hamilton, has been observed at scale in US municipal politics, and is technically replicable by any organized advocacy group with access to current generative AI tools. As of the date of this paper, no Ontario municipality has published a documented procedure for detecting, responding to, and publicly accounting for AI-driven communication campaigns.

This paper proposes the Council Astroturfing Response Procedure, a five-element municipal framework that is consistent with the Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario / Ontario Human Rights Commission Joint Principles for Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence (January 2026), the Ontario Trustworthy AI Framework (December 2024), and the upcoming MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments (July 2026 and January 2027). The procedure is designed to be adopted by Council resolution, operationalized by the Clerk's office and the AI Governance Lead, and reported transparently to the public.

The paper is structured to support direct adaptation by Ontario municipal staff. The procedural elements are reproduced in full at §5. Annexes provide implementation templates and an objection-response framework. The procedure is offered openly for municipal use.

## 1. Introduction: A new category of municipal harm

Municipal councils in Ontario, like elected bodies everywhere, are designed around the assumption that constituent communications are authentic. The presumption is that an email to a councillor reflects, at minimum, the considered view of a single identifiable person who took the time to write it. The procedural protections of municipal deliberation — public meetings, deputations, advance distribution of agendas, transparency of vote records — all build on this foundation.

Generative AI has substantially weakened that assumption. As of 2026, an advocacy organization with modest technical resources can produce hundreds or thousands of substantively distinct, individually-styled emails to a target council in a matter of hours. The emails can be calibrated to local context, can vary tone and register, and can pass casual inspection for authenticity. The cost has collapsed from the previously high friction of organizing a real letter-writing campaign to something approaching zero.

This is not a hypothetical concern. By mid-2026, multiple Ontario municipal councils had received communications later identified as AI-generated, in policy contexts including climate action, housing density, short-term rental enforcement, and zoning. The Thorold incident is the most fully documented, but it is not the only one. It is the leading edge of a class of harm that municipal procedure was not designed to absorb.

The regulatory environment has only recently begun to address this. The IPC-OHRC Joint Principles for Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence (January 21, 2026) explicitly contemplate AI systems institutions "use or are exposed to," which is the operative language for the present analysis. The Ontario Trustworthy AI Framework (December 1, 2024) addresses AI risk management at the provincial-services level but its principles are referenced and used as benchmarks by IPC enforcement of MFIPPA. The MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments (effective July 1, 2026 and January 1, 2027) impose mandatory Privacy Impact Assessment, safeguards, and binding-order authority for the Information and Privacy Commissioner.

None of these instruments directly mandates a council astroturfing response procedure. But all of them create the conditions in which the absence of such a procedure becomes a documented compliance gap, and in which the adoption of one becomes the most defensible response to a documented class of harm.

This paper makes the case that Ontario municipalities have a procedural duty to adopt, in the near term, a framework that is consistent with the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles, that is robust against the threat model, and that preserves rather than constrains legitimate constituent advocacy. The Council Astroturfing Response Procedure described herein is the Sentainel Compliance Research Group's contribution to that effort.

## 2. The Thorold incident: A public-record case study

### 2.1 What happened

In late 2024 and through 2025, Thorold City Council was the subject of a coordinated email campaign opposing the Town's participation in the Partners for Climate Protection program (PCP). PCP is a long-running joint program of the Federation of Canadian Municipalities and ICLEI-Local Governments for Sustainability that supports municipal greenhouse gas reduction action.

The campaign was reported by Canada's National Observer (April 30, 2026), with earlier coverage in CBC and The Pointer, as having been organized by a climate-policy advocacy group using customized chatbots to generate the bulk of the email volume. The chatbots produced emails calibrated to appear as individual constituent communications: variation in phrasing, tone, length, and specific points of emphasis. Council, as deliberative bodies routinely do, treated the volume as a signal of constituent sentiment.

In a 7-1 vote, Council resolved to withdraw the Town of Thorold from PCP.

### 2.2 What this case demonstrates

The Thorold incident is procedurally significant for five reasons.

First, it is the most fully documented public-record case in Ontario of an AI-driven communication campaign producing a measurable change in municipal policy outcome. The chain of causation between AI-generated communications and the policy decision is reported, sourced, and on the public record. This is rare; most suspected AI campaigns are not investigated publicly and the causal link is harder to establish.

Second, the campaign succeeded despite operating under municipal procedural rules that, in principle, should have defended against it. Council received the communications, councillors reviewed them, the matter was debated, and the vote was held. No procedural step was skipped. The harm occurred within and through normal democratic process — which is exactly the threat model the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles contemplate.

Third, no party to the incident — not Council, not the Clerk, not staff — failed in any identifiable way. The councillors who voted to withdraw acted in good faith on what they understood to be a signal of constituent sentiment. The Clerk processed communications as the Clerk normally processes communications. Staff did their work. The procedural infrastructure was simply not designed for the threat.

Fourth, Thorold Council members were direct victims, not perpetrators. This is important framing. Any procedural response should be designed to support and protect councils against a class of attack that targets their legitimacy, not to suggest that any specific Council acted improperly.

Fifth, the incident has placed a public-record duty on every Ontario municipality. With the Canada's National Observer reporting now in the regulatory and journalistic record, no Ontario council can credibly claim ignorance of the threat. Going forward, the absence of a procedural response is itself a public-record fact that will be referenced if a similar incident produces a similar outcome elsewhere.

### 2.3 What this case does not demonstrate

The Thorold incident does not demonstrate that Thorold Council made a substantively wrong policy decision. Withdrawal from PCP is a defensible policy choice on its own merits, and authentic constituents may share the concerns surfaced by the synthetic campaign. The harm at issue is procedural — the decision-making process was influenced by inputs that did not represent what they appeared to represent — and the procedural harm exists independently of whether the policy outcome was correct.

The incident also does not demonstrate that AI-generated communications are inherently illegitimate. Many forms of legitimate advocacy use AI as a drafting and amplification tool, including translation, accessibility support for constituents with disabilities, and the assistance of advocacy organizations whose individual members fully endorse the communications sent on their behalf. The procedural concern is not AI involvement per se. The procedural concern is synthetic communication volume that misrepresents the underlying scale and authenticity of constituent sentiment.

This distinction matters because the procedural response must preserve, not constrain, legitimate constituent advocacy in all its forms.

## 3. The Ontario regulatory stack and its application to municipal communication processes

### 3.1 The four-layer stack

Four regulatory layers now apply to AI governance at Ontario municipalities. None of them directly require a council astroturfing response procedure. All of them create the conditions in which the absence of one becomes a documented compliance gap.

**Layer 1: MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments.** The Municipal Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act, amended by Schedule 6 of Bill 97 (the *More Homes Built Faster Act, 2022*), introduces mandatory Privacy Impact Assessments for institutions, expanded safeguards requirements, mandatory breach notification, and binding-order authority for the Information and Privacy Commissioner. The PIA, safeguard, and breach-notification provisions take effect on July 1, 2026. The IPC binding-order authority and the whistleblower provisions take effect on January 1, 2027.

The IPC has stated publicly that its assessments of institutional compliance will be "grounded" in the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles. This is the operative link between MFIPPA hard-law enforcement and the principles-level framework described below.

**Layer 2: IPC-OHRC Joint Principles for Responsible Use of Artificial Intelligence.** Published jointly by the Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario and the Ontario Human Rights Commission on January 21, 2026, the Principles are not statutory but are the operational standard against which the IPC will assess institutional AI use under MFIPPA. The seven principles are: Valid and Reliable; Safe; Privacy Protective; Human Rights Affirming; Transparent; Accountable Governance; Accountable Recourse.

Principle 5 (Transparent) and Principle 7 (Accountable Recourse) are the two principles most directly relevant to the council astroturfing scenario. Principle 5 requires that institutions maintain transparent decision-making processes that the public can understand and trust. Principle 7 requires that institutions establish mechanisms for individuals and groups to challenge AI-related institutional outcomes. Both are difficult to satisfy in a council deliberation context where synthetic communications are present but not identified.

**Layer 3: Ontario Trustworthy AI Framework and Responsible Use of AI Directive.** Adopted on December 1, 2024, the Framework and Directive govern AI use across the Ontario Public Service. They do not directly bind municipalities. However, the Framework is referenced as a benchmark in the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles, and many of its operational requirements (ops-1 through ops-6) are now treated as good-practice baselines for municipal AI governance. The Framework's risk-management requirement (ops-2) is the operative requirement for the present analysis.

**Layer 4: EDSTA Bill 194.** The *Strengthening Cyber Security and Building Trust in the Public Sector Act, 2024* received Royal Assent on November 25, 2024. Its AI provisions are not yet proclaimed in force as of the date of this paper. When proclaimed, EDSTA will impose obligations on "public sector entities" including municipalities for AI governance, transparency, human oversight, and risk management. The Bill is significant for the present analysis because it explicitly contemplates regulations imposing additional AI governance requirements; council astroturfing response is the kind of governance gap such regulations may address.

Overlaying all four layers, the Ontario Human Rights Code section 9 prohibits discrimination on Code-protected grounds in the provision of services. Where a synthetic communication campaign targets policies affecting Code-protected groups, the failure to detect and respond becomes a potential Code concern in itself.

### 3.2 How the stack applies to council deliberation

Municipal council deliberation is, in privacy and AI compliance terms, a personal-information-processing activity. The Clerk processes constituent communications. Council members read them. Decisions are influenced by them. The institutional record retains them. Under MFIPPA and the IPC-OHRC Principles, the institution has corresponding obligations regarding accuracy, integrity, transparency, and accountability.

Where synthetic communications are part of the input, three specific compliance concerns arise.

First, **integrity of the deliberative record.** The institutional record of council deliberation includes the communications received, the matters debated, and the resolutions adopted. If the communications were generated by AI for the purpose of influencing the outcome, the deliberative record contains material misrepresentation. Under MFIPPA s. 30 (accuracy and integrity of personal information) and the IPC-OHRC Principle 1 (Valid and Reliable), the institution has a duty to maintain records of integrity. Failure to detect known categories of misrepresentation is a documented gap against that duty.

Second, **transparency to the public regarding decision basis.** Under IPC-OHRC Principle 5 (Transparent), institutions must maintain decision-making processes that the public can understand. If a council vote was substantially influenced by communications later shown to be synthetic, the public's ability to understand the decision basis is impaired. This is a transparency harm independent of whether the decision was substantively correct.

Third, **Code-protected group recourse.** Under IPC-OHRC Principle 7 (Accountable Recourse) and Human Rights Code s. 9, where institutional decisions affect Code-protected groups, those groups must have access to mechanisms to challenge the decisions and to understand how they were arrived at. If a synthetic communication campaign influenced a decision affecting a Code-protected group, the group's ability to challenge the decision is impaired by the absence of any procedural detection or response.

The council astroturfing response procedure described in §5 is designed to address all three concerns directly.

## 4. The threat model: How AI-driven communication campaigns work

### 4.1 Generic campaign architecture

As of 2026, an AI-driven communication campaign against a municipal council typically has the following architecture. The description here is sourced from public reporting of the Thorold, Hamilton, and US municipal incidents, and from published research on synthetic political communication.

**Stage 1: Targeting.** The campaign organizer identifies a specific municipal council, a specific upcoming decision, and a specific desired outcome. The council is selected on the basis of policy exposure, electoral cycle, perceived persuadability, and resource constraints. Smaller municipalities with shorter agendas and fewer staff are over-represented as targets because the impact-per-effort is higher.

**Stage 2: Persona construction.** The organizer constructs synthetic personas calibrated to appear as authentic local constituents. Persona variables include neighbourhood, demographic plausibility, profession, prior civic engagement (often falsified to imply attendance at past meetings or familiarity with local figures), and policy emphasis. A modest campaign uses dozens of personas; a larger one uses hundreds.

**Stage 3: Content generation.** The organizer uses generative AI (GPT-class, Claude-class, or open models) to produce emails in each persona's voice. Modern models produce sufficient variation in syntax, vocabulary, and emphasis that surface-level inspection by Clerk staff or councillors does not reliably detect synthesis. The content is calibrated to specific policy talking points and is often grounded in real local context drawn from publicly available municipal documents.

**Stage 4: Delivery.** Emails are delivered to councillors' public email addresses, often staged over hours or days to simulate organic uptake. Some campaigns also deploy social media amplification, comments on local news sites, and (in well-resourced campaigns) coordinated deputations from a smaller subset of in-person participants who are authentic but lend perceived legitimacy to the broader synthetic volume.

**Stage 5: Outcome capture.** The campaign organizer monitors the council decision. If the desired outcome is achieved, the campaign is logged as successful and the persona/content infrastructure is retained for future use. If the outcome is not achieved, the infrastructure is iterated.

### 4.2 Why detection is difficult

Detection is difficult for three reasons.

First, the content quality is high. Modern generative AI produces emails that are linguistically plausible, syntactically varied, and contextually grounded. The era of detection by linguistic fingerprint (the early-2020s ChatGPT "telltale" patterns) is largely over for an organizer with even modest technical skill.

Second, the volume is calibrated below intuitive thresholds. A campaign of 100 to 200 emails over 5 to 10 days is sufficient to influence a municipal council decision but is rarely large enough to trigger casual suspicion. Councillors are accustomed to receiving volume on contested issues. The signal-to-noise ratio is exactly what the campaign organizer wants.

Third, councils have no internal incentive to detect. A council member who suspects communications are synthetic faces an awkward question: what is the procedural response, and what are the political costs of being wrong? Without a documented procedure, the safest individual action is to treat all communications as authentic and let the chips fall.

This third factor is the most important. The absence of a documented procedure is itself a structural defence-suppressor. Detection improves dramatically once a documented procedure exists.

### 4.3 Signals that distinguish synthetic from authentic input

Although the bar for detection is rising, signals do exist. The following list is drawn from research on synthetic political communication and from analysis of public-record campaigns.

**Signals consistent with synthetic origin:**

- Substantial volume of substantively similar communications received in a compressed time window, typically 100 or more in 72 hours, calibrated to fall just before a council decision
- Senders without prior communication history with the Town and without matching service-record presence (water bills, tax accounts, business licenses)
- Phrasing patterns that mirror talking points or argument structures published by an identifiable external advocacy organization
- Sender email domains and address patterns that do not match Town voter rolls or typical municipal correspondent demographics
- Surface-level civic literacy with no engagement on actual local procedural detail (no knowledge of staff names, prior council resolutions, or specific Town context that authentic engaged constituents would possess)
- Lack of follow-up engagement when councillors respond directly; the sender does not reply, attend the meeting, submit a deputation, or otherwise persist in the engagement
- Communications received from senders whose IP-derived geographic indicators do not match the Town or its commuter region

**Signals consistent with authentic origin:**

- Variety in tone, length, register, phrasing, and specific points of emphasis that goes beyond what AI calibration typically produces
- Engagement with specific local context — street names, neighbours, local businesses, attendance at prior council meetings, knowledge of which staff member handles which file
- Follow-up dialog after councillor response — answering questions, providing additional detail, persisting in the engagement
- Co-occurrence with attendance at council meetings, deputations, or community group activity
- Pre-existing relationship with council members, staff, or the Town (prior FOI requests, prior service requests, prior advocacy on related matters)

No single signal is dispositive. The procedure described in §5 is designed to flag combinations of signals for further review, not to make binary authenticity determinations on individual communications.

### 4.4 The asymmetry problem

A structural feature of the threat is that the cost of campaign generation has collapsed while the cost of campaign detection has not. An advocacy organization can produce a calibrated 200-email campaign in a few hours of AI-tool work for a marginal cost approaching zero. A municipality attempting to detect that campaign requires either staff time at the Clerk's office (expensive and procedurally awkward) or specialized tooling (which, as of 2026, does not exist as a productized offering for the municipal sector).

This asymmetry is the underlying reason why a procedural rather than a technological response is the right framing. The procedure does not require expensive tooling. It requires a documented protocol, a designated owner, and a transparent reporting mechanism. The asymmetry of cost is partially neutralized by making the response procedural and replicable across municipalities.

## 5. The Council Astroturfing Response Procedure (the artifact)

The procedure consists of five elements. Each is presented in operational form suitable for direct adaptation by an Ontario municipality. Elements are numbered for cross-reference, not for sequencing — most are operationalized in parallel.

### 5.1 Communication Intake Logging

All written communications received by Council and Council members in their capacity as elected officials are subject to a standard intake log maintained by the Clerk's office or by the designated AI Governance Lead. The log records, for each communication:

- Date and time of receipt
- Sender email address and (where available without compromising privacy) sender IP region (province or country)
- Subject matter, classified against a controlled vocabulary of current council policy files
- Whether the sender has prior communication history with the municipality on any matter
- Whether the sender has any documented service-record presence (e.g., property tax account, business license, water utility account) — this check is conducted at an aggregate level only, never disclosing the individual record content
- Whether the communication is substantively similar to other communications received in the prior 30 days, assessed by an unsupervised similarity measure (TF-IDF cosine similarity or equivalent) executed by the AI Governance Lead

The intake log is an internal administrative record. It is not published in raw form. It is the input to the analytical procedures in §5.2 and the public reporting in §5.4.

The log is established for all communications, not only suspected synthetic ones. This is critical for two reasons. First, it provides a baseline against which deviations can be assessed. Second, it avoids the procedural impropriety of inspecting only communications that some staff member has subjectively flagged as suspicious — a pattern that would itself raise Charter and equity concerns.

### 5.2 AI Governance Lead Pattern Review

The AI Governance Lead, designated under IPC-OHRC Principle 6 (Accountable Governance), reviews the intake log on a defined schedule and at defined triggers. The Lead is typically a senior staff member at the Clerk's office, the CAO's office, or the IT department, with cross-departmental authority and a published role description.

The defined schedule is weekly during periods of normal council activity and continuously (within one business day of receipt) during the 30 days preceding any council decision on a matter that has received 25 or more communications in the prior 30 days.

The defined triggers, any of which initiate a Pattern Review in addition to the schedule, are:

- A council file receives more than 25 communications in 30 days, or more than 100 communications in 7 days
- Any individual communication or pattern is flagged by Council member or staff as potentially synthetic
- A council file is publicly identified by a credible external organization (provincial agency, recognized advocacy group, media outlet) as the subject of a coordinated communication campaign
- The AI Governance Lead, exercising professional judgment, identifies a pattern warranting review

The Pattern Review applies the signal framework in §4.3 to the intake log entries for the relevant council file. The Review produces a written assessment, with one of three outcomes: (a) no significant indicators of synthetic origin, (b) some indicators of synthetic origin warranting Council notice but no procedural intervention, (c) significant indicators of synthetic origin warranting Council notice and consideration of the deliberation pause in §5.3.

The Pattern Review is documentary, not adversarial. It does not name individual senders. It does not characterize individual communications as inauthentic. It characterizes patterns at the aggregate level. The deliberative basis for any procedural response is the pattern, not the individual.

### 5.3 Council Notice and Deliberation Pause Option

Where a Pattern Review produces outcome (b) or (c), the AI Governance Lead provides a written Council Notice in advance of the council deliberation on the affected matter. The Notice is included in the council package with the same standing as a staff report.

The Notice includes:

- A factual description of the communication volume, time distribution, and any aggregate-level signals consistent with synthetic origin
- A clear statement that the Notice does not, and is not intended to, characterize any individual communication as inauthentic
- A clear statement that authentic constituents may share the underlying policy concerns regardless of campaign origin, and that those concerns warrant Council's substantive consideration
- An assessment of whether the deliberation should proceed on schedule or whether Council may wish to consider a one-meeting pause under the Deliberation Pause Option

The Deliberation Pause Option authorizes the Mayor, with the concurrence of the CAO or the Clerk, to recommend that Council defer the deliberation by one meeting cycle to allow further intake analysis. The Pause is procedural, not substantive. It does not change Council's authority over the matter. It does not bind Council to any particular outcome. It does not prevent any constituent from continuing to communicate with Council during the Pause period.

The Pause Option is exercised sparingly. Most Pattern Reviews will produce outcome (a) or (b), with no Pause. The Pause is reserved for cases where the indicators are strong, the matter is significant, and further analysis would meaningfully improve the integrity of the deliberation.

### 5.4 Public Quarterly Reporting

The AI Governance Lead publishes a public quarterly report on the Town's website summarizing the operation of the procedure during the quarter. The report includes:

- Total communications received
- Number of Pattern Reviews triggered
- Number of Pattern Reviews producing each of outcomes (a), (b), (c)
- Number of Council Notices issued
- Number of Deliberation Pauses exercised
- Aggregate description (not naming files or senders) of patterns identified
- Any external advocacy organizations whose campaigns were referenced in Pattern Reviews
- Average time from intake to Pattern Review

The quarterly report serves three functions. It satisfies the transparency obligation under IPC-OHRC Principle 5. It deters synthetic campaigns by creating reputational cost for campaign organizers. It provides empirical data that other Ontario municipalities can use to calibrate their own procedures.

The report is published at a permanent URL on the Town's website and is included by reference in the Town's MFIPPA annual report.

### 5.5 IPC and OHRC Coordination

Where a Pattern Review identifies signals consistent with an AI-driven campaign that has the characteristics of an attempt to influence municipal decision-making on a matter affecting Code-protected groups, the AI Governance Lead refers the matter to both the Information and Privacy Commissioner of Ontario and the Ontario Human Rights Commission for guidance.

The referral is documentary and consultative, not a complaint. It informs the IPC and the OHRC that a pattern has been identified, provides the Pattern Review assessment, and requests guidance on procedural response. The referral is published in the quarterly report at the aggregate level (number of referrals, regulatory body referred to) but the substantive content of the referral is confidential between the municipality and the regulator.

The IPC and OHRC do not, as of the date of this paper, have published guidance specific to AI-driven communication campaigns. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group anticipates that such guidance will develop as referrals accumulate. Early-adopting municipalities should expect to be partially constructing the regulatory practice through their referrals.

## 6. Implementation guidance

### 6.1 Adoption pathway

The procedure is adopted by Council resolution. A model resolution is provided at Annex A.

The recommended adoption pathway has three stages.

**Stage 1: Administrative endorsement (week 1-2).** The Mayor, CAO, and Clerk review the procedure and endorse it for Council consideration. The endorsement is documentary, not formal; it establishes that the senior administrative officials have reviewed the procedure and recommend it.

**Stage 2: Pilot operation (week 3-14).** The AI Governance Lead, designated at the Stage 1 endorsement, operates the procedure on a pilot basis for 90 days. During the pilot, Pattern Reviews are conducted and Council Notices are issued as the schedule and triggers require, but the Deliberation Pause Option is not exercised. The pilot produces an initial quarterly report, drafted but not yet published, that demonstrates the procedure in operation.

**Stage 3: Council adoption (week 15).** Council considers the procedure, the pilot quarterly report, and the model resolution. The resolution formally adopts the procedure, authorizes the Deliberation Pause Option, and directs publication of the quarterly report. Adoption is by simple majority vote.

### 6.2 Resource implications

The procedure is designed to operate within the existing administrative capacity of a typical Ontario municipality. The marginal staff time is estimated at:

- 2-4 hours per week for the intake log maintenance, by Clerk's office staff
- 4-8 hours per Pattern Review, by the AI Governance Lead
- 8-12 hours per quarter for the quarterly report, by the AI Governance Lead
- 2-4 hours per Council Notice, by the AI Governance Lead

For a municipality the size of Thorold (approximately 22,000 residents), the total annual marginal staff time is estimated at 200-400 hours. This is within the discretion of existing staff allocation and does not require new hiring.

The procedure does not require specialized software. Microsoft Lists, SharePoint, or equivalent municipal records management tooling is sufficient. The similarity-measure analysis (§5.1, last bullet) can be performed in Excel or Python by any staff member with intermediate technical skill.

For municipalities that wish to operate the procedure with reduced internal staff time, commercial platforms including the Sentainel Municipal Edition provide the intake log, Pattern Review, quarterly report, and IPC/OHRC referral workflow as a managed service. See §12.

### 6.3 Training and role design

The AI Governance Lead role does not require AI-specific technical training. The role requires familiarity with the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles, the MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments, and the Ontario Human Rights Code. Most senior Clerks, Privacy Officers, and senior IT staff in Ontario municipalities have the requisite familiarity.

Where the Lead is new to AI compliance frameworks, the recommended training pathway is:

1. Reading the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles (approximately 4 hours)
2. Reading the Ontario Trustworthy AI Framework and Directive (approximately 6 hours)
3. Reading the relevant MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments and IPC commentary (approximately 4 hours)
4. Reading this paper and its annexes (approximately 3 hours)
5. Attending AMO and AMCTO sessions on AI governance as available

Total training time: approximately 17-20 hours, achievable within a single work week.

Clerk staff supporting the intake log require approximately 2 hours of orientation. Council members do not require training; the procedure is designed so that Council's responsibilities are limited to reading Council Notices and exercising judgment on the Deliberation Pause Option when recommended.

### 6.4 Integration with existing municipal records management

The intake log integrates with the municipality's existing records management. Communications received by Council are already, in most Ontario municipalities, subject to a records-retention schedule under the *Municipal Act*. The intake log adds metadata to those records; it does not change the records themselves and does not change the retention schedule.

For municipalities using Microsoft 365 environments, the integration is operationally straightforward: a SharePoint list or Microsoft List with the required intake fields, populated either manually or via automated mail-flow rules from the Clerk's mailbox. For municipalities using other environments, equivalent tooling is available.

The intake log is itself subject to MFIPPA. It contains personal information of correspondents. It is therefore retained, secured, and disposed of according to the municipality's MFIPPA records-management policies. Where a member of the public makes an FOI request for the intake log under MFIPPA s. 36, the request is processed normally, with personal-information redactions applied per MFIPPA s. 14 as warranted.

## 7. Anticipated objections and responses

The procedure described above will face several objections. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group has anticipated the following.

### 7.1 "This is censorship."

*Objection:* The procedure inspects and characterizes constituent communications. This is a censorship mechanism dressed in compliance language.

*Response:* The procedure does not restrict, alter, suppress, or refuse to receive any communication. Every communication received is logged. Every communication received is forwarded to Council in the normal course. Every constituent retains their full ability to communicate with Council. The procedure characterizes communication patterns at the aggregate level for the purpose of preserving the integrity of Council's deliberative process. It does not characterize individual communications. It does not act on individual communications. Its operational outputs — the Pattern Review, the Council Notice, the Deliberation Pause Option — affect Council's own procedural awareness, not the public's ability to speak.

A mechanism that informs Council that it has received 200 substantively similar emails in 72 hours from senders without service-record presence is not censorship. It is procedural integrity.

### 7.2 "This is partisan."

*Objection:* The procedure will be selectively applied to campaigns from one ideological direction.

*Response:* The procedure applies equally to communication patterns from any ideological direction. The signals at §4.3 are content-agnostic; they describe pattern characteristics, not policy positions. The quarterly report at §5.4 names external advocacy organizations whose campaigns were referenced in Pattern Reviews, regardless of those organizations' ideological alignment.

The procedure is more likely, in practice, to be triggered by well-organized, well-resourced campaigns than by poorly-organized ones. Well-organized campaigns exist on every part of the political spectrum. The procedure does not advantage or disadvantage any particular spectrum position. Municipalities adopting the procedure should publish their first quarterly report with explicit attention to the cross-spectrum application to forestall partisan criticism.

### 7.3 "This treats constituents as suspects."

*Objection:* The default position of municipal democracy should be to treat constituent communications as authentic. This procedure inverts that default.

*Response:* The procedure does not invert the default. The default remains that all communications are forwarded to Council in the normal course and considered as constituent input. The procedure adds an aggregate-level pattern analysis that is invisible to individual constituents and that does not affect how their individual communications are processed.

The default of treating individual communications as authentic is preserved precisely because the procedure is aggregate. Individual constituents are not, at any point, treated as suspects. Patterns of communication, in aggregate, may be characterized for purposes of preserving deliberative integrity. This is a categorical distinction.

### 7.4 "AI is not actually being used in these campaigns, this is overstated."

*Objection:* The threat is overstated. Most municipal communication campaigns are organic constituent advocacy. Procedural responses to AI-driven campaigns will produce false positives against legitimate advocacy.

*Response:* The procedure is designed to be highly tolerant of false positives. The default outcome of a Pattern Review is outcome (a), no significant indicators. Even outcome (b) produces only a Council Notice, with no procedural intervention. Only outcome (c) — significant indicators across multiple signal categories — produces consideration of the Deliberation Pause Option, and even that is procedural deferral, not substantive intervention.

The procedure is calibrated for the case where the threat is real but specific instances are uncertain. The cost of a false positive is a Council Notice that says, in effect, "Council should be aware that this file has received unusually concentrated communication volume." That is not a high-cost false positive. The cost of a false negative — failing to identify a synthetic campaign that influences a decision — is structurally higher.

The Thorold incident, on the public record, demonstrates that the threat is not overstated.

### 7.5 "This is solving a problem that does not exist at our municipality."

*Objection:* The Thorold incident is one case. Most Ontario municipalities have not experienced an AI-driven campaign. The procedural cost is not justified by the actual risk.

*Response:* The cost-benefit asymmetry in §4.4 makes this argument structurally weak. The cost of campaign generation is approaching zero. Any municipality with a contested upcoming decision is a potential target. The cost of the procedure is bounded (200-400 staff hours per year for a municipality the size of Thorold). The expected cost of a successful undetected campaign — measured in policy outcome change, public-record reputational harm, IPC inquiry exposure, and erosion of constituent trust in council process — is substantially larger than the procedural cost.

Additionally, the public-record duty established by the Thorold incident applies to all Ontario municipalities, not only those that have themselves been targeted. The IPC's enforcement posture under MFIPPA Bill 97 is forward-looking, not retroactive. Municipalities that have not adopted any procedural response by July 1, 2026 will be assessed against the IPC-OHRC Principles in the context of a known threat that has been on the public record for more than 14 months.

### 7.6 "We do not have the staff capacity."

*Objection:* The procedure assumes administrative capacity that smaller municipalities do not have.

*Response:* The procedure is intentionally designed for the staff capacity of a small Ontario municipality. Thorold, with approximately 22,000 residents and a small administrative staff, is the design reference. Municipalities of comparable size can operate the procedure within existing staff allocation; 200-400 hours per year is well within ordinary administrative discretion.

Where staff capacity is genuinely constrained, three mitigations apply. First, the intake log maintenance is the only continuous-time element; everything else is event-triggered. Second, the procedure can be operated with reduced frequency (monthly Pattern Reviews rather than weekly) by smaller municipalities, with a corresponding documented adjustment. Third, commercial platforms including the Sentainel Municipal Edition reduce the marginal staff time substantially for municipalities that prefer a managed-service approach.

The staff-capacity objection is a real consideration but not a barrier to adoption.

### 7.7 "The procedure does not stop the harm."

*Objection:* Even if a Pattern Review identifies a synthetic campaign, Council can still vote however it chooses. The procedure does not actually prevent campaign-influenced outcomes.

*Response:* This is correct. The procedure does not constrain Council's substantive decision-making authority. It is not designed to. Council remains the elected body and retains full discretion.

The procedure produces three different forms of value that are independent of outcome change:

First, **informed deliberation.** Council members making decisions with awareness of a documented synthetic campaign pattern make better-informed decisions than Council members without that awareness. Even if the substantive outcome is unchanged, the informed decision is more defensible.

Second, **public-record integrity.** The decision is recorded with the procedural context. Future analysis, including any subsequent IPC inquiry, has access to the full deliberative record. This protects Council from later criticism for outcomes made without awareness of campaign patterns.

Third, **deterrent value.** Campaign organizers' incentive structure depends on cost-effective influence. Public quarterly reporting of identified campaigns increases the cost (in reputational and operational terms) of running campaigns against municipalities that operate the procedure. This is a structural deterrent, not a tactical one.

The procedure is a procedural integrity instrument, not a substantive policy instrument. Its purpose is to ensure that whatever Council decides, the decision is made on the basis of an informed and defensible record. That is sufficient.

## 8. Limitations and scope

The procedure as described is subject to the following limitations, which the Sentainel Compliance Research Group acknowledges directly.

**Limitation 1: The procedure does not detect all synthetic campaigns.** A sophisticated campaign organizer who anticipates the procedure can calibrate communication volume, timing, sender patterns, and content variation to evade the Pattern Review signals. The procedure raises the bar for successful campaigns; it does not eliminate the possibility of successful campaigns. As detection techniques and adversarial techniques co-evolve, this paper will be revised. Subsequent versions are anticipated.

**Limitation 2: The procedure addresses email-driven campaigns most clearly.** Campaigns that operate through other channels — social media, comments on local news sites, paid advertising, in-person organizing — are less directly addressed by the intake log mechanism. The procedure can be extended to those channels, but doing so requires additional design work that is outside the scope of this paper.

**Limitation 3: The procedure assumes good-faith municipal operation.** A municipality that uses the procedure as cover for partisan targeting, for retaliation against legitimate advocacy, or for selective application against disfavoured groups will produce harm. The procedure's safeguards — content-agnostic signals, aggregate analysis, public quarterly reporting, IPC and OHRC coordination — are designed to make bad-faith operation difficult and visible. They do not make it impossible. Adopting municipalities should consider Council oversight mechanisms (annual review of procedure operation, external audit) to maintain operational integrity.

**Limitation 4: The procedure does not address AI use within the municipality.** Where the municipality itself uses AI in its decision-making processes — for example, an AI chatbot processing constituent communications before they reach Council — additional procedural frameworks are required. Such use is addressed in the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles directly and in separate Sentainel research outputs. See §12.

**Limitation 5: The regulatory landscape is in flux.** The IPC-OHRC Joint Principles are recent. MFIPPA Bill 97 amendments are not yet in force. EDSTA Bill 194 AI provisions are not yet proclaimed. As the regulatory environment matures, the procedure may require adjustment to remain aligned. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group commits to publishing revisions as the regulatory landscape evolves.

**Limitation 6: This paper is not legal advice.** Nothing in this paper constitutes a legal opinion or replaces the advice of qualified counsel. Municipalities adopting any aspect of the procedure should retain Ontario municipal law counsel and, where appropriate, MFIPPA, OHRC, and Charter specialists to confirm specific implementation choices.

## 9. Comparative analysis: Adjacent jurisdictions and analogous procedures

### 9.1 United States

U.S. municipal politics has experienced AI-driven communication campaigns at substantially larger scale than Canadian municipalities. Documented cases include synthetic comment campaigns against the Federal Communications Commission (the 2017 net-neutrality comment campaign, retroactively identified as substantially synthetic), state-level legislative campaigns in multiple states, and an emerging pattern of city-council targeting in mid-size American cities.

The U.S. response has been fragmented. No federal procedural framework exists. State-level responses are limited. Municipal-level responses are early-stage and not standardized. The closest analogue to the procedure described in this paper is the City of San Francisco's 2024 "public comment authenticity" pilot, which uses similar signal-based pattern detection at the City Clerk level. The San Francisco pilot is not, as of mid-2026, codified by ordinance.

The Canadian advantage in this domain is the regulatory infrastructure provided by the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles and MFIPPA Bill 97. Ontario municipalities have a more coherent regulatory framework to build on than U.S. municipalities, and can adopt a more developed procedural response.

### 9.2 European Union

The EU AI Act, in force since August 2024 with high-risk provisions effective August 2026, does not directly address council astroturfing but does establish AI transparency obligations that apply to the deployers of AI systems used to influence democratic processes. Article 50 of the EU AI Act imposes disclosure obligations on generators of synthetic media. Where such media is used in a campaign against an EU member-state municipal council, Article 50 obligations apply to the generator, and the municipality has a corresponding consumer-protection angle for response.

No EU member state has, as of the date of this paper, published a council-level procedural response of the kind described here. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group is in informal contact with municipal AI governance researchers in Netherlands and Estonia who have indicated interest in adapting the procedure for EU contexts.

### 9.3 Quebec

Quebec's Law 25, in force from September 2023 with the final mandatory provisions effective September 2024, addresses AI use by Quebec public bodies at a level comparable to Ontario's MFIPPA Bill 97. Quebec municipalities have a corresponding interest in council astroturfing response. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group is preparing a parallel French-language version of this paper, adjusted for Quebec's specific regulatory context and Law 25 references, for publication in late 2026.

### 9.4 Analogous procedures: Public comment integrity at higher levels of government

The procedure described here has analogues at higher levels of government in Canada. Health Canada, the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission, and Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada have all published, to varying degrees, public-comment authenticity protocols for their consultation processes. These protocols are not directly transferable to municipal contexts because they assume regulatory-agency resources and authority, but they share design DNA: signal-based pattern detection, aggregate analysis, public reporting, agency-level integrity protection.

Municipal procedural responses are at an earlier stage of development than agency-level responses. This paper is offered as one step in the process of catching up.

## 10. Conclusion

The Ontario municipal sector faces a documented and replicable category of harm: AI-driven communication campaigns directed at council deliberation. The harm is procedural — it operates within and through ordinary municipal process — and the existing procedural infrastructure was not designed to absorb it. The Thorold incident, on the public record since April 2026, establishes the threat as actual and the regulatory exposure as forward-looking.

The regulatory stack — MFIPPA Bill 97, the IPC-OHRC Joint Principles, the Ontario Trustworthy AI Framework, EDSTA Bill 194 — does not directly mandate a procedural response, but creates the conditions in which the absence of one becomes a compliance gap.

The Council Astroturfing Response Procedure described in this paper is one approach to closing that gap. It is designed to be adopted by Council resolution, operationalized by existing staff, and reported transparently to the public. It is content-agnostic, non-partisan, non-censorial, and preserves rather than constrains legitimate constituent advocacy. It is calibrated for the staff capacity of small Ontario municipalities. It produces value independent of substantive outcome change. It places the municipality in a defensible position under all four layers of the Ontario regulatory stack.

No municipality has yet adopted the procedure in the form described. The Sentainel Compliance Research Group offers the procedure publicly for any Ontario municipality that finds it useful. The first municipality to adopt it will be a citation reference for the others.

This paper will be revised as the threat landscape, the regulatory environment, and the operational experience of adopting municipalities evolve. Comments, corrections, and adaptations are welcomed at the contacts listed at §12.

## Annex C: Pattern Review Template

The following template structure is recommended for Pattern Reviews produced by the AI Governance Lead.

```
PATTERN REVIEW [PR-YYYY-NNNN]
Council File Reference: [reference]
Period Under Review: [start date] to [end date]
Prepared by: [AI Governance Lead, name and role]
Date of Review: [date]

1. COMMUNICATION VOLUME
   Total communications received in period: [n]
   Communications received in compressed window: [n, e.g., past 72 hours]
   Comparable baseline volume for similar files: [n, with sources]

2. SENDER ANALYSIS (AGGREGATE)
   Senders with prior communication history: [n] of [n] (percentage)
   Senders with service record presence: [n] of [n] (percentage)
   Senders with out-of-region IP signal: [n] of [n] (percentage)
   Sender email domain distribution: [summary]

3. CONTENT ANALYSIS (AGGREGATE)
   Mean similarity score across communications: [float]
   High-similarity clusters identified: [n]
   External advocacy organization talking points identified: [yes/no, organization name(s) if yes]
   Local civic literacy assessment: [aggregate description]

4. ENGAGEMENT FOLLOW-UP
   Communications producing follow-up dialog with council members or staff: [n]
   Communications with attendees at related council meetings or deputations: [n]

5. ASSESSMENT
   Outcome: [a, b, or c]
   Rationale: [narrative, 2-4 paragraphs]
   Council Notice recommended: [yes/no]
   Deliberation Pause recommended: [yes/no]
   IPC referral recommended: [yes/no]
   OHRC referral recommended: [yes/no]

6. EXPLICIT STATEMENTS
   This Pattern Review does not, and is not intended to, characterize any individual communication as inauthentic.
   The substantive policy concerns raised in the communications received warrant Council's full consideration regardless of campaign-pattern indicators.

7. ATTACHMENTS
   [Intake log entries for the period]
   [Any external reference materials]
```

## Annex D: Glossary

- **AI Governance Lead**: The senior staff member designated under IPC-OHRC Principle 6 with cross-departmental authority for AI governance at the municipality.
- **Communication Intake Log**: The internal administrative log of communications received by Council, maintained per §5.1.
- **Council Notice**: The written briefing provided to Council in advance of a deliberation where a Pattern Review produces outcome (b) or (c), per §5.3.
- **Deliberation Pause Option**: The procedural option authorized at §5.3 for the Mayor, with the concurrence of the CAO or Clerk, to recommend a one-meeting deferral.

- **Pattern Review**: The aggregate-level analysis conducted by the AI Governance Lead per §5.2, producing one of three outcomes (a, b, c).
