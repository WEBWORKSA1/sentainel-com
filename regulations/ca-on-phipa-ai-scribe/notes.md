# Ontario PHIPA + AI Scribe Guidance — Strategic Notes

## Why PHIPA is Sentainel's highest-value Ontario regulation

Three reasons:

1. **Active enforcement.** Unlike EDSTA (no private duty of
   care) or Bill 149 (limited to ESA), PHIPA has all three
   teeth: AMPs ($50K/$500K), criminal prosecution ($200K/$1M),
   and civil action ($10K mental anguish). The IPC issues
   AMPs and the courts uphold them (see Decision 298,
   2025 ONSC 5208).

2. **Broadest scope.** PHIPA reaches both public and private
   sector HICs. Every Ontario hospital, every family practice,
   every pharmacy. Total addressable market: ~30,000 HICs
   province-wide.

3. **Clear IPC guidance.** The January 28, 2026 AI Scribe
   Guidance gives Sentainel a regulator-defined checklist to
   sell against. Other Ontario regulations require
   interpretation; PHIPA + AI Scribe = run the IPC's list.

## The vendor training prohibition

Effectively absolute under PHIPA. The IPC's reading:

- PHIPA permits secondary use only with consent OR
  de-identification to "very low" re-identification risk
- Audio recordings almost never meet the de-identification
  standard (voice is identifying)
- Therefore: vendor cannot use HIC data to train its models

This is a CONTRACTUAL flag for every AI scribe procurement.
Sentainel's vendor due diligence template includes explicit
"no training" clauses.

## The Otter.ai incident as compliance teaching tool

September 2024: an unapproved AI scribe joined a virtual
hepatology rounds meeting at an Ontario hospital. The AI was
installed on a departed physician's personal device, used a
personal email never rescinded from the calendar invite,
joined the meeting, recorded PHI of 7 patients, and emailed
transcripts to 65 recipients including 12 former employees.

The IPC response letter (December 2024) recommended:
- Block unapproved AI tools at the firewall level
- Audit offboarding procedures to revoke all calendar/meeting
  access
- Maintain AI inventory + procurement gate
- Train staff on shadow AI risks

Sentainel's shadow-AI module is built around exactly this
playbook. Every Ontario HIC customer gets:
1. Firewall ruleset templates blocking common unapproved AI
2. Offboarding checklist covering calendar + meeting + SaaS
3. Quarterly shadow-AI scan of expense reports + browser
   activity (where customer permits)

## Open interpretive questions

1. **Does the IPC's "informed express consent" position apply
   to AI tools that don't record audio (e.g., text-only NLP
   tools processing clinical notes)?** Likely yes for any
   tool with vendor involvement and secondary-use risk.

2. **Are AI scribes "agents" under PHIPA s. 6 or "service
   providers" under common-law contracting?** The IPC has
   not definitively resolved this. Sentainel positions
   either reading the same way: full contractual due diligence
   required.

3. **What is "very low" re-identification risk?** The IPC has
   not quantified it. Practitioner consensus: <0.05 probability
   of re-identification with no aggregation.

4. **How does PHIPA interact with EDSTA when both apply
   (hospitals)?** EDSTA layers on top of PHIPA. Where both
   require an accountability framework, a single integrated
   framework satisfies both. PHIPA breach notification (60
   days) is faster than any anticipated EDSTA breach
   notification.

These should be flagged for the customer's outside counsel
during onboarding.
