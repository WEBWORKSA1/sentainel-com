required_action: |
  (b) A governmental entity may not develop or deploy an artificial
  intelligence system for the purpose of uniquely identifying a
  specific individual using biometric data or the targeted or
  untargeted gathering of images or other media from the Internet
  or any other publicly available source without the individual's
  consent, if the gathering would infringe on any right of the
  individual under the United States Constitution, the Texas
  Constitution, or state or federal law.

  (c) A violation of § 503.001 is a violation of this section.

  "Biometric data" per § 552.054(a) means data generated by
  automatic measurements of an individual's biological
  characteristics, including fingerprints, voiceprints, retina/iris
  patterns, or other unique biological identifiers. EXCLUDES:
  - Physical or digital photographs (and derivatives)
  - Video/audio recordings (and derivatives)
  - HIPAA-covered information for healthcare treatment, payment, or
    operations
plain_language_summary: |
  Two distinct prohibitions in § 552.054:

  1. GOVERNMENTAL ENTITIES (§ 552.054(b)): Cannot deploy AI for
     biometric identification that involves gathering images/media
     from public sources without consent IF the gathering would
     infringe constitutional or statutory rights. Clearview-style
     facial recognition built on public scraping is the target.

  2. ALL PERSONS via § 552.054(c) + § 503.001 cross-reference:
     Existing Texas biometric privacy law (Capture or Use of
     Biometric Identifier Act, CUBI) applies with full force, and
     any CUBI violation is automatically a TRAIGA violation —
     stacking penalties.

     CUBI (§ 503.001) requires:
     - Notice to individual before capturing biometric identifier
       for commercial purposes
     - Consent before capture
     - Destruction within one year after the purpose ends
     - No sale, lease, or disclosure without consent

  AMENDED BY HB 149: § 503.001 was amended to clarify that public
  internet availability of an image is NOT consent for biometric
  identifier extraction (§ 503.001(b-1)). This closes the
  "Clearview loophole."

  AI training carveout: AI model training using biometric data is
  exempted UNLESS the system is "used or deployed for the purpose
  of uniquely identifying a specific individual" (§ 503.001(e)(2)).
  If you train then deploy for identification, the carveout
  evaporates (§ 503.001(f)).

  CRITICAL FOR AI:
  - Facial recognition AI for security, access control, or fraud
    prevention triggers § 503.001 obligations
  - Voice biometric AI for authentication triggers § 503.001
  - Behavioral biometric AI (keystroke dynamics, gait analysis)
    may trigger if "uniquely identifying"
  - AI vendors using Texas resident images scraped from social
    media for training face § 503.001(b-1) exposure
  - Healthcare biometric AI is carved out per HIPAA exception

penalty_for_violation: |
  TRAIGA penalties under § 552.105 apply: $10K-$12K curable,
  $80K-$200K uncurable, $2K-$40K/day continuing.

  STACKING: CUBI penalties under existing § 503.001 are PER-PERSON
  ($25,000 per occurrence under Tex. Bus. & Comm. Code § 503.001,
  enforced by AG). A § 503.001 violation is automatically a TRAIGA
  violation, so both penalty regimes apply.

  Aggregate exposure for AI vendors training on Texas resident
  images: $25,000 (CUBI) + $80,000-$200,000 (TRAIGA) per affected
  Texas resident. For a model trained on 100,000 Texas-resident
  images, theoretical maximum exposure exceeds $20 billion.

ambiguity_notes: |
  - The CUBI training carveout language is somewhat circular — you
    can train on biometric data, but if you deploy for
    identification, the carveout disappears AND you also became
    subject to the § 503.001 destruction/consent rules retroactively.
    Conservative practice: assume the carveout doesn't apply.
  - Behavioral biometrics (keystroke timing, mouse dynamics) sit
    at the edge of "unique biological pattern." Less-clearly-
    biological than fingerprints but functionally uniquely
    identifying. Probably captured but no clear case law yet.
  - The "publicly available source" element of § 503.001(b-1) is
    a direct response to Clearview AI / PimEyes-style models. AI
    vendors who can't document training data provenance face
    significant exposure.
  - Whether photographs of Texas residents posted to Facebook or
    Instagram constitute "publicly available source" is now
    resolved against the scraper: § 503.001(b-1) clarifies that
    public availability is NOT consent.
